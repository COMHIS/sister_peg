Title : David Hume to Richard Davenport
Date : Monday, 27 April 1767
Written from:  Greater London, England, United Kingdom
Written to : no location of receipt
Editorial project : EE founding corpus
Published online : 2008

London 27th of April 1767

Dear Sir:

The affair of M. Rousseau's pension is now finally concluded. I had to day a letter from M. Bradshaw1 of the treasury, informing me that the Duke of Grafton2 was instantly to order a hundred pounds a year to be paid, without deductions, to any person whom he should order to receive it. It is to commence from the first of this month, and will, I suppose, be paid quarterly. He has nothing to do but to write a common missive letter to any person, banker or other, empowering him to receive payment as often as it becomes due.

Have you seen a little book, published within these few days, being an account of Rousseau's writings and conduct?3 It is a high panegyric on him; but without attempting to throw any blame upon me: on the contrary, it owns he is in the wrong in his quarrel with me. It is said to be the work of Dr. Sterne;4 but it exceeds even the usual extravagance of that gentleman's productions.

Lord Holdernesse5 told me that he intended to send a person across the country in order to take a view of your plough and its operations; I doubt not but you will give him a good reception.6

I am, dear Sir, your most obedient humble servant,

David Hume
