Title : David Hume to Adam Smith1
Date : Wednesday, 27 February 1754
Written from:  Edinburgh, Edinburgh (unitary authority), Scotland
Written to : no location of receipt
Editorial project : EE founding corpus
Published online : 2008
Crossref DOI : http://dx.doi.org/10.13051/ee:doc/humedaOU0030035b1c

Dear Sir

I am writing kind of circular Letters, recommending Mr Blacklock's2 Poems3 [to]a all my Acquaintance, but especially to those, whose Approbationb wou'd contribute most to recommend them [to]a the World. They are, indeed, many of them very elegant, and wou'd have deserv'd much Esteem, had [t]heya come from a Man plac'd in the most favorable Circumstances. What a Prodigy are they, when considerd as the Production of a man, so cruelly dealt with, both by Nature [and]a Fortune? When you add to this, that the Author is a Man of the best Dispositions, that I have ever known, and tho' of great Frugality, is plac'd in the most cruel Indigence, you will certainly think his Case more deserving of Pity & Regard than any you have almost met with. Mr Foulis4 has Copies to dispose of, which I have sent him; & which he will disperse without expecting any Profit. I must entreat you, not only to take a Copy yourself, but also to take a few more & [dis]posea of among your Acquaintance. I trust at least to have half a dozen disposd of by your [me]ans.a I have sold off above fifty in a few days. The Price is three Shillings. That you may [rec]ommenda them with a safer Conscience, please read the Ode to a young Gentleman going to the Coast of Guinea, that on Refinements in metaphysical Philosophy, that to a Lady on the Death of her Son; the Wish, an Elegy; the Soliloquy. I am much mistaken, if you do not find all these [v]erya laudable Performances; & such as wou'd be esteem'd an Ornament to Dodesley's Miscellanies [o]r even to better Collections.5

We expected to have seen you in Town about this time; but have been dissappointed. I am [v]erya glad your Health has been so well confirmd this Winter, as I hear it has been. My Compliments to Mr & Mrs Betham.6 If that Lady can be engag'd to have an Esteem of Mr Blacklock's Productions, she wou'd be of great Service in dispersing them. Tho born blin[d,]a he is not insensible to that Passion, which we foolish People are apt to receive first by th[e]a Eyes; and unless a man were both blind & deaf, I do not know how he cou'd be altogether secure of Impressions from Mrs Betham.7 I am Dear Sir

Yours sincerely

David Hume

Edinr 27th of February 1754
