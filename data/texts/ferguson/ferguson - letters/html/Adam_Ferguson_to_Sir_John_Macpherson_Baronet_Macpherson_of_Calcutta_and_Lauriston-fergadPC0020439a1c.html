Title : Adam Ferguson to Sir John Macpherson, Baronet Macpherson of Calcutta and Lauriston
Date : Tuesday, 3 July 1798
Written from:  Hallyards on Manor Water, near Peebles, Scottish Borders, Scotland
Written to : no location of receipt
Editorial project : EE founding corpus
Published online : 2013
Crossref DOI : http://dx.doi.org/10.13051/ee:doc/fergadPC0020439a1c

[folio 1 recto]

Hallyards 3rd July 1798

My Dear Friend

My Silence is not negligence nor forgetfulness if I had ten thousand of the best letters that ever were written you should have them all: but what I can write from this post, at which my prime consolation is that I have nothing to do but to wait quietly till my time comes. The French I trust, altho they may tease, cannot subdue this Armed nation; and all Speculation on the Subject is at an end. I have in my view a most delightful Kirkyard retired & green on the bank of a running Water1 & faceing a Verdant hill which in your part of the World would pass for a tremendous mountain: but to me it gives the Idea of Silence & Solitude away from the noise of folly. And so I fancy myself laid there with a Stone to tell the Rustic Moralist2 what [folio 1 verso] he will not understand; because I sometimes project it should bea in Greek as follows: ως εγω τον ϰοσμον εθαυμασα ϰαι συ θεασαμενος χαιϱε3 but then Again I wish to explain it. And so it should be. I have seen the Works of God, it is now your turn. Do you behold them and rejoice4. I would speak my verve for Agriculture in Greek also Ανθϱωπος ιχνος γεοϱγια5 And you may judge of my willingness to write when I put all this on paper to you.

I have not stirred from home for many months past, till lately, when Admiral & Mrs Nugent being at Edinburgh, led me thither to gratify my sense of their kindness to my little Seaman6. And I am Still the more Convinced that Nugent is the most amiable faultless Creature upon Earth. In that excursion I met our Friend Sr John Sinclair7 in the Street: this put it in his head to write to me since my return hither, an Account of works he is projecting to promote what heb calls Statistical Philosophy. I hinted that his project is too vast: but he tells me that his mind [folio 2 recto] is made up, to draw it on a great Scale and on as perfect a plan as possible. And that he never Startled at any difficulty that could be Surmounted ever Since he collected as far back as the year 1780 One thousand & two hundred men & in one days time made a road of Six miles long over a mountain till then thought impassiblec 8. The fact is that he has got an instinct to be doing which other people ought to know how to employ without turning him out of place. Altho I have so many excuses for writing so seldom, I am not willing to Allow you any, so I pray when you are writing however let there be a Scrap for me even if you should not be able to tell me what is become of Buonaparte9. By the by is that a genuine Prussiand paper10 in answer to the French demands, which we have in the news paper. It is menacing and I do not see how the great Nation can give way to it without appearing to be cowed. They certainly meant to gall us & to secure the Co-operation of Prussia against us by transferring Hanover11

&c &c your most Affectionately
A. F.
